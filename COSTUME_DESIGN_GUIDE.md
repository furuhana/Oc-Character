# Costume Design Guide

This guide shapes costume judgment for character attributes and final image prompts.
It is not a fixed outfit library. Use it to decide why a character wears something,
how the outfit supports the body and story, and how to avoid repeating the same
silhouette.

## Core Taste

- The outfit must look good first. Function and world logic should make the design
  more attractive, not plain or merely realistic.
- The preferred body appeal is a very bulky muscular adult male with a fitted torso
  layer: extremely broad shoulders, heavy chest, thick arms, strong thighs, and
  rounded power mass. Keep the torso readable through clothing.
- Treat this as the body floor for adult male characters unless the user explicitly
  asks for an average, slim, or delicate build.
- A tight white top is strongly preferred: fitted white T-shirt, fitted sleeveless
  undershirt, fitted long-sleeve shirt, or another clean white close-fitting top.
  It should visibly fit around the chest, shoulders, and arms in clean cel shading.
- The fitted top should usually be tuckable or visibly tucked into pants, overalls,
  apron waist, harness, or belt structure.
- If the user asks to remove outerwear, do not replace it with a vest, open shirt,
  short jacket, coat, or overshirt. Let the tight white top become the main upper
  body read.
- The outfit should stay clean anime concept art, not photoreal fashion rendering.

## Required Costume Reasoning

Before writing costume fields or final image prompt outfit text, decide these points:

- Life scene: where the character is usually seen.
- Movement needs: standing, running, riding, climbing, carrying, desk work, care work,
  stealth, fighting, or social performance.
- Social visibility: public uniform, hidden identity, faction display, civilian cover,
  or private off-duty look.
- Body framing: whether the outfit emphasizes the thick torso, broad shoulders,
  forearms, waist, or lower-body weight.
- Layering structure: single layer, open layer over fitted top, vest, apron, harness,
  overalls, long coat, sleeves, rain layer, or partial uniform.
- Personal trace: one detail that comes from habit, memory, vanity, repair, poverty,
  relationship, or private taste.
- Fantasy anchor: one real garment part or accessory that carries the unusual power.
- Anti-repetition swap: what silhouette, layer, color logic, or accessory is different
  from recent characters.

## Inputs That Should Influence Clothing

### Numerical Attributes

- Wealth affects fit, repair, fabric neatness, custom details, and whether the outfit
  is new, old, second-hand, uniform-issued, or carefully maintained.
- Danger affects protective parts: cuffs, gloves, boots, knee panels, hidden padding,
  quick-release straps, tool placement, and low-noise fasteners.
- Desire affects display: tighter fit, brighter accents, jewelry, exposed arms,
  polished shoes, decorative belt buckles, or intentionally flattering proportions.
- Violence tendency affects readiness: shorter hems, grippy shoes, reinforced wrists,
  spare fasteners, less dangling decoration, and easier access to tools or weapons.
- Discipline affects neatness: tucked tops, symmetrical straps, closed pockets,
  polished details, and controlled color placement.

### World And Faction

- Factions may have house marks, logos, badges, embroidery, color rules, rank trims,
  issued garments, forbidden colors, or mass-produced uniform pieces.
- Rank changes clothing quality and permission: lower ranks wear practical issued
  pieces; senior members may have custom cut, cleaner fabric, rare buttons, better
  shoes, or quieter insignia.
- A hidden or former faction member may keep removed badge marks, covered embroidery,
  mismatched uniform pieces, or a single private token.
- World setting can create clothing restrictions: weather, district rules, school or
  office dress codes, medical hygiene, municipal safety rules, nightlife fashion, or
  gang territory symbols.

### Combat And Action

- Close combat usually prefers short hems, secure cuffs, tucked tops, grip-friendly
  shoes, reinforced wrists, and minimal loose cords.
- Ranged tools need shoulder stability, reachable pouches, balanced straps, clear
  arm movement, and stable footwear.
- Support, healing, control, or ritual roles can use pockets, sleeves, tags, small
  instruments, charms, gloves, aprons, or document holders.
- Stealth favors low-noise cloth, matte anime color blocks, covered metal, fitted
  bags, dark fasteners, and no swinging ornaments.
- Long coats, robes, scarves, or dangling parts are allowed when justified by split
  hems, hidden snaps, magnetic closures, ceremonial status, or non-combat context.

## Silhouette Rules

Each outfit needs one clear silhouette change point. Do not let every character become
short jacket plus fitted white T-shirt plus utility pants.

Useful silhouette change points:

- fitted white top tucked into high-waist trousers
- sleeveless white undershirt with open shirt or vest
- fitted white long-sleeve with rolled outer sleeves
- apron front panel over thick torso and waist, but treat this as a high-repetition
  choice for service, archive, pharmacy, florist, kitchen, and shop roles; do not
  use it again if recent characters already used apron straps, apron fronts, or
  waist-covering cloth panels
- suspenders or harness crossing the chest
- overalls with one strap loose or both straps tight
- long coat with open front showing the fitted top
- short uniform shirt worn open over the fitted top
- knit cardigan, work vest, or formal waistcoat
- wide trousers, tapered trousers, cuffed pants, or tucked boots
- sleeve guards, arm bands, shoulder wrap, or protective half-cape

## Accessory Rules

Accessories must have a reason. Pick one or two strong accessories instead of many
generic items.

Accessory sources:

- work necessity: ID card, keys, stamp, pen, pouch, meter, scanner, gloves
- faction identity: badge, rank trim, embroidered mark, issued belt, color stripe
- private memory: pendant, repaired button, old scarf, borrowed jacket, child sticker
- body habit: rolled sleeves, wrist tape, tucked towel, worn shoe heel, favored cap
- fantasy anchor: glove palm, sleeve cuff, belt buckle, chest badge, shoe sole,
  collar tag, inner pocket, button, zipper pull, embroidery line

## Material Language

Describe materials in anime-design terms, not photoreal texture terms.

Prefer:

- clean cotton-like white fabric
- flat color blocks
- simple cel-shaded folds
- tidy uniform cloth
- soft knit shape
- matte rain layer as a simple translucent anime shape
- clean leather-like belt
- polished but simplified metal accent

Avoid overusing:

- hyper-detailed fabric grain
- realistic sweat, oil, pores, dust, mud, rust, grime
- glossy photoreal latex, plastic, or wet leather
- complex material reflections
- fashion-photo lighting language

If a shiny accent is needed, keep it small and graphic: a badge, button, buckle,
pendant, charm, or tool edge. Do not make the whole outfit shiny.

## Color Logic

Do not let element, ability, faction, or occupation keywords directly flood the
full outfit palette. A water character should not automatically become all blue;
a fire character should not automatically become all red; a plant character should
not automatically become all green.

Build color decisions in this order:

1. Wearable base colors first.
2. Stable lower-body colors second.
3. Profession, faction, weather, or social context colors third.
4. Element, magic, or ability colors last, usually as accents.

Default palette ratio:

- 60% base wearable color: black, charcoal, warm gray, off-white, navy-black,
  olive gray, dark brown, muted khaki, or other low-saturation clothing colors.
- 30% secondary clothing color: coat, vest, shirt layer, uniform panel, apron,
  harness, trousers, or other large but controlled garment area.
- 10% element, fantasy, or accent color: badge, cuff line, stitching, lining,
  jewelry, tool, weapon, low-glow mark, seal, charm, gauge, rune, or small magical
  detail.

Element colors should usually appear on:

- tools, weapons, stamps, meters, gauges, seals, charms, or signature props
- badges, rank marks, embroidery, buttons, buckles, zipper pulls, or belt hardware
- gloves, sleeve trim, inner lining, small stitching, or one controlled garment edge
- low-glow marks, magical scales, tiny runes, or restrained aura details

Element colors should not default to:

- entire trousers
- entire shoes
- all major clothing layers
- full-body monochrome or near-monochrome palettes
- every accessory at once

Shoes are the grounding color. Default footwear should use black, charcoal, dark
brown, gray, off-white, muted workwear colors, or very dark navy. Use element-colored
shoes only when the character concept specifically needs it, such as sportswear,
a formal uniform rule, a ceremonial identity, or a deliberate loud fashion choice.

The white fitted top is the clean body-reading anchor. It should be supported by
surrounding colors rather than buried in a one-note palette.

Do not default every outfit to navy, charcoal, brass, and black boots. Vary the
base and secondary colors, but keep the final outfit wearable and attractive before
adding element color.

Before finalizing costume fields, ask:

- If the magic or element color were removed, would the outfit still look good?
- Do the pants and shoes stabilize the design instead of copying the element color?
- Is the fantasy color limited to readable anchors, trims, tools, or low-glow marks?
- Does the palette have enough contrast around the white fitted top?

## Anti-Repetition Checks

Before finalizing, check whether the outfit repeats too many recent defaults:

- tight white T-shirt
- short jacket
- charcoal utility pants
- black commuter boots
- fingerless gloves
- wide utility belt
- brass or amber signature item
- apron, apron front, apron straps, apron-like archive straps, florist apron,
  kitchen apron, pharmacy apron, or any large cloth panel covering the waist/belly

The tight white top can stay. If it stays, vary at least two other major elements:
layering structure, pants silhouette, footwear, accessory source, faction marking,
color logic, or fantasy anchor location.

## Example Reasoning Patterns

### Night Pharmacist

Avoid only writing pharmacy jacket plus utility pants, and avoid defaulting to an
apron if recent service characters already used one. Decide whether he needs quiet
movement, hygiene, long standing, drawer access, and a calm public face. Keep a fitted
white top tucked into soft trousers, a vest, suspenders, or a compact belt structure.
Possible variations include a short pharmacy cardigan, sleeve guards, soft indoor
shoes, key cord, stamp pouch, or medicine tag. The fantasy anchor can live on a
measuring spoon, cuff mark, chest badge back, belt pouch, or pocket tag.

### Lost-Property Archivist

The outfit should show careful sorting and memory work, not just office uniform.
Use a fitted white top as the base, then choose a vest, cardigan, sleeve guards,
high-waist trousers, suspenders, a document strap, or a compact crossbody case.
Avoid defaulting to an apron or apron-like front panel unless the character concept
specifically depends on one. Accessories can be index tags, old keys, a ticket clip,
repaired button, or bookmark charm. The fantasy anchor can be an archive tag,
bookmark, glove fingertip, belt notebook, or crossbody case clasp.

### Municipal Patrol Worker

Start from weather, walking distance, public authority, and risk level. Keep the
fitted white top visible under a rain layer, vest, harness, or open uniform shirt.
Vary with reflective trims, waterproof over-trousers, rubber boots, shoulder radio,
sealed pouches, or district badge. The fantasy anchor can be a rain gauge, boot sole
mark, badge stripe, or sealed cuff.

### Faction Member In Civilian Cover

The outfit should read like normal streetwear at first. Keep the fitted white top
tucked into pants, then hide the faction through stitch color, lining, ring, button,
inside-collar mark, belt hardware, or removed badge scar. Avoid large obvious logos
unless the character wants to be recognized.
